The Identification Barrier

CPC 1908 · Private International Law

The claimant must identify a defendant with sufficient particularity to effect service of process. Company X may be in one jurisdiction, Company Y in another, servers in a third. Indian private international law has no rule for jurisdiction over cross-border agent delegation chains.

Defence Fails — 2 Grounds

① No Free Will

Novus actus requires a free, deliberate human will. Agent B has no independent will — it executed a delegated objective. An algorithmic output is not a voluntary intervening act.

② Foreseeability

Company X built a system with open-ended delegation and no budget guardrails. Commissioning an unconstrained agent was entirely foreseeable — foreseeability independently prevents causal severance.

Liability Laundering

The Structural Risk

If both X and Y's defences succeed simultaneously, the victim has no remedy. Each party points to the other. This is Liability Laundering — deliberate organisation of multi-agent architectures to ensure no single entity bears proximate responsibility. No existing Indian or EU instrument addresses this.

The Principal Chain Doctrine

Core Principles

01 / Unified Standing

Joint & Several Liability

All deployers in the chain are co-defendants. The victim recovers in full from any one defendant — without identifying the specific proximate error.

Salomon v Salomon [1897] AC 22

Separate corporate personalities preserved. This is concurrent liability — not veil-piercing. Contribution between co-defendants governed by existing principles.

02 / Presumption of Linkage

Rebuttable Presumption

The initial delegating agent's deployer is presumed responsible for downstream harm. Burden shifts to the developer — who holds the architectural blueprint.

Analogous to s.113B Indian Evidence Act

Cannot be introduced by judicial innovation. Requires express statutory enactment under CPC 1908 / IEA 1872. Addresses the evidentiary opacity of black-box architectures.

03 / Handshake Guardrails

Standard of Care

Developers must implement budget limits, scope constraints, and human approval triggers. Failure = breach of standard of care. Compliance = safe harbour from Chain liability.

Anti-Avoidance Requirement

Contractual exclusion clauses require statutory anti-avoidance — analogous to UCTA 1977 or s.2(r) Consumer Protection Act 2019.
