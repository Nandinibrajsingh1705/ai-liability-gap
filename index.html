04. The Distributed Liability Gap

Current civil liability frameworks require a direct and proximate cause. When AI agents interact across legal entities, this chain of causation fragments into a "Black Box."

⚖️

The User

Defense: Causal Severance
"My intent was for 100 units. The sub-agent's decision to buy 10,000 units is an emergent property outside my contemplation. Under traditional tort law, this breaks the chain of proximate cause."

🤖

The "Finger-Pointing" Vacuum

In existing literature (Springer, 2024), the single-actor model fails here. The EU's Product Liability Directive struggles because software isn't always a "product," and the AI Liability Directive preserves the burden on the victim to identify which specific agent erred. Because the harm emerges from the interaction, no single entity is proximately responsible.

05. The Solution

Bridging the gap through Causal Unity.

The Principal Chain Doctrine

1. Unified Accountability

The law treats the entire delegation chain as a single joint unit. The victim sues the chain, removing the procedural barrier of identifying the "faulty" sub-component.

2. Presumption of Causation

Harm emerging from autonomous inter-agent interaction is presumed linked to the initial delegator. This effectively blocks "Liability Laundering" across corporate entities.

3. Doctrine Impact

  • Ensures Victim Redress: Bypasses the "Causal Fog" that currently leads to zero-recovery outcomes in courts.
  • Incentivizes Safety: Forces developers to implement "Handshake Guardrails" to avoid shared chain liability.
  • Legal Harmonization: Provides a consistent framework for cross-border agentic interactions.

"This model ensures that distributed causation does not result in zero compensation, turning a legal vacuum into a regime of systemic accountability."
